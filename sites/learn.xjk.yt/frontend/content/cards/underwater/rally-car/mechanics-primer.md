# Rally Underwater Mechanics Primer

Rally underwater is best understood as a speed-retention and contact-state car. It can hold useful speed while partially floating, it can exploit penalty surfaces with the right inputs, and it stays stable enough that odd wall, edge, and reactor tricks become practical.

This page is a hub. It should be short enough to share, but connected enough that a reader can jump into the specific mechanic they actually need.

This card is based on DIVE Discord notes from Pholtur, Reddnox, Patriam, Mulga, Cache_Up, and follow-up discussion. Treat exact speed values and surface claims as useful working knowledge until we add clips, maps, and repeatable tests.

## Baseline behavior

- Rally forward and backward tend to top around **100 speed** on normal surfaces underwater.
- Forward driving accelerates better.
- Backward driving has stronger grip.
- A quick left-right steering input at the start can improve acceleration on flat start blocks.
- Oddly shaped starts, such as sausage-like starts, may not support the same start trick.

The important part is not just the number. The car has enough underwater stability that it can turn partial contact into a technique instead of a failure.

## The big model

Rally underwater has three recurring patterns:

1. Keep speed by changing how the car contacts the block.
2. Use steering rhythm to resist penalty speed loss.
3. Use the car's stability to convert floatiness into controlled jumps, ledge grabs, and transition tools.

## Surface sketch

Road, dirt, grass, and wood appear to behave similarly: the basic plan is usually to take turns inside while avoiding unnecessary sliding. Ice and plastic are special.

Ice can be driven with Rally-style ice slides. Plastic is unusually strong because Rally underwater accelerates well on it, keeps speed even through small oversteer, and can retain overspeed longer than other surfaces.
